# What Are Capital Expenses (CapEx)?

Capital expenses (CapEx) are the funds an organization uses to acquire, upgrade, and maintain physical assets such as property, industrial buildings, or equipment. In a business context, an expense is considered CapEx when it provides a benefit that extends beyond the current tax year, effectively increasing the capacity or "useful life" of a fixed asset.

### The Strategic Role of CapEx in Maintenance
For maintenance managers and facility operators in 2026, the distinction between capital expenses and operating expenses (OpEx) is a critical tool for financial communication. While OpEx covers the day-to-day costs of keeping a plant running—such as utility bills, minor repairs, and routine labor—CapEx represents a strategic investment in the future. When you propose a major overhaul of a production line or the installation of a facility-wide sensor network, you are speaking the language of CapEx.

To act as a "CFO Whisperer," maintenance leaders must demonstrate how these capital outlays reduce the **Total Cost of Ownership (TCO)**. By capitalizing a major equipment purchase, the cost is not hit all at once on the profit and loss statement; instead, it is spread out over several years through depreciation. This allows for better cash flow management and improved financial ratios, which are vital for securing executive approval for high-ticket items.

### Key Related Terms

### Operating Expenses (OpEx)
Unlike CapEx, OpEx refers to the ongoing costs required to run a business. In maintenance, this includes consumable spare parts, preventive maintenance labor, and software subscriptions. Understanding the "Capitalization Threshold"—the dollar amount at which an expense must be treated as CapEx rather than OpEx—is essential for accurate budgeting.

### Asset Depreciation
Depreciation is the accounting method used to allocate the cost of a tangible asset over its useful life. For industrial machinery, this reflects the wear and tear and eventual obsolescence of the equipment. High-performing maintenance teams use data to prove that their strategies extend the useful life of assets, thereby slowing depreciation and maximizing the original capital investment.

### Return on Investment (ROI)
Every capital expense is scrutinized for its potential return. In an industrial setting, this is often calculated by the reduction in downtime, increased throughput, or energy savings generated by the new asset. According to standards set by organizations like the [National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST)](https://www.nist.gov), strategic investment in advanced manufacturing technology is a primary driver of long-term industrial competitiveness.
