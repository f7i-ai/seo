### 2. DETAILED EXPLANATION: The Three Pillars of Industrial Compliance

To understand "compliance def" in practice, one must look at the intersection of three critical domains: Regulatory, Operational, and Technical.

#### A. Regulatory Compliance Management
This involves adhering to laws set by governing bodies. In the United States, this primarily concerns [OSHA safety standards](https://www.osha.gov) and, for specific industries like life sciences or food production, **21 CFR Part 11**. 

21 CFR Part 11 is a critical "compliance def" component regarding electronic records and signatures. It requires that digital systems used in manufacturing are secure, traceable, and tamper-proof. Factory AI automates this by generating immutable logs for every [asset management](/features/asset-management) action, ensuring that when an auditor asks for proof of maintenance, the data is instantly available and verified.

#### B. The Maintenance Audit Trail
A "compliance def" is incomplete without a robust maintenance audit trail. This is a chronological record that provides documentary evidence of the sequence of activities that have affected at any time a specific operation, procedure, or event. 

In a [CMMS software](/products/cmms-software) environment, this means:
*   Who initiated the work order?
*   What [PM procedures](/features/pm-procedures) were followed?
*   Which parts from [inventory management](/features/inventory-management) were used?
*   What was the final outcome and who signed off on it?

#### C. ISO Standards: 55000 and 9001
ISO 55000 provides the global "compliance def" for asset management, while ISO 9001 focuses on quality management. Achieving compliance with these standards requires a shift from reactive maintenance to a [predictive maintenance](/products/predict) model. Factory AI facilitates this by providing the data-driven insights necessary to prove that assets are being managed optimally to prevent failure, rather than just repairing them after they break.

#### D. Common Pitfalls in Industrial Compliance Definition
Even with the best intentions, many facilities fail to meet their "compliance def" due to three recurring mistakes:
1.  **The "Paper-to-Digital" Gap:** Many plants still use paper checklists that are manually entered into a system at the end of the week. This creates a "lag" in compliance data, making it impossible to prove real-time safety adherence during a surprise inspection.
2.  **Lack of Calibration Tracking:** Compliance often fails because the tools used to measure asset health (like vibration meters or thermal cameras) are themselves out of calibration. Factory AI solves this by including calibration schedules within the [asset management](/features/asset-management) module.
3.  **Incomplete SOPs:** A work order that simply says "Fix Motor" is not compliant. A compliant work order must include specific [PM procedures](/features/pm-procedures), torque specifications, and safety clearance steps.

#### Real-World Scenario: The F&B Compliance Challenge
Consider a mid-sized Food & Beverage (F&B) plant. Compliance here involves EHS (Environment, Health, and Safety) standards, FDA regulations, and strict uptime requirements. If a conveyor motor fails, it’s not just a production issue; it’s a compliance risk. 
Using [predictive maintenance for conveyors](/solutions/predictive-maintenance-conveyors), Factory AI identifies early signs of bearing wear. It automatically triggers a [corrective action report](/features/work-order-software) and schedules maintenance before a safety hazard occurs. This "closed-loop" system is the gold standard for compliance in 2026.
