### The Definitive Answer: What Are the Maintenance Record Keeping WHS Requirements in Australia?

In Australia, **maintenance record keeping WHS requirements** are primarily governed by the **Model Work Health and Safety (WHS) Regulations**, specifically **Regulation 237**. This regulation mandates that a person with management or control of plant at a workplace must keep a record of all tests, inspections, maintenance, commissioning, decommissioning, dismantling, and alterations carried out on the plant.

To be compliant and "audit-proof" in 2026, these records must:
1.  **Be kept for the period the plant is used or until the person relinquishes control of the plant.**
2.  **Be accessible to any person who is likely to operate the plant.**
3.  **Detail specifically *what* work was carried out, *when* it was done, and *who* performed it (the "competent person").**

While paper logbooks were once common, they are now considered a liability due to the risk of loss, damage, and lack of searchability. The current industry standard for compliance is a **Computerized Maintenance Management System (CMMS)** that creates an immutable digital audit trail.

### Detailed Explanation: Navigating the Regulatory Landscape

#### The Legal Framework: Regulation 237 and Beyond
The core of maintenance compliance in Australia is the harmonization of WHS laws. While most states and territories (NSW, QLD, SA, TAS, ACT, NT, and WA) have adopted the Model WHS Laws, Victoria operates under the *Occupational Health and Safety Act 2004* and *OHS Regulations 2017*. However, the practical requirements for maintenance record keeping are remarkably similar across all jurisdictions: **if you own it, you must maintain it, and you must prove you maintained it.**

**Regulation 237 (Records of Plant)** specifically states that records must be kept for:
*   **Registered Plant:** Equipment that requires design or item registration (e.g., pressure vessels, cranes, lifts).
*   **High-Risk Plant:** Machinery where failure could result in catastrophic injury.

However, best practice dictates that *all* production assets should have a documented lifecycle. If an accident occurs involving a conveyor belt or a compressor, Safe Work Australia or the relevant state regulator (e.g., WorkSafe Victoria, SafeWork NSW) will immediately request the maintenance history.

#### The "Audit-Proof" Standard
"Keeping records" is not enough; your records must be "audit-proof." In the event of a serious incident, an investigator will look for gaps in the timeline.
*   **Scenario:** A motor fails, causing a fire.
*   **The Audit:** The investigator asks for the maintenance logs from the last 24 months.
*   **The Failure:** You have paper logs for 20 months, but a 4-month gap where the logbook was lost or coffee was spilled on it. This is a compliance breach.
*   **The Solution:** A digital system like [Factory AI's CMMS software](/products/cmms-software) ensures that every inspection is timestamped and stored in the cloud. Even if a technician forgets to file a report, the system flags the missed PM (Preventive Maintenance), creating a record of the *process* management.

#### The Role of the "Competent Person"
Australian regulations frequently refer to a "competent person." This is someone who has acquired through training, qualification, or experience the knowledge and skills to carry out the task.
Your maintenance records must verify *who* did the work. A signature on a paper tag is often illegible. Modern platforms allow you to assign specific [preventive maintenance procedures](/features/pm-procedures) only to technicians with the verified digital credentials to perform them. This adds a layer of verification that paper simply cannot match.

#### Integrating Predictive Maintenance with Compliance
In 2026, compliance has moved beyond "fix it when it breaks." Regulators look favorably upon organizations that utilize **Predictive Maintenance (PdM)**. By monitoring asset health in real-time, you demonstrate a higher duty of care.
For example, using [predictive maintenance for compressors](/solutions/predictive-maintenance-compressors) or [pumps](/solutions/predictive-maintenance-pumps) generates a continuous stream of health data. This data serves as a secondary compliance record, proving that the machine was operating within safe parameters (vibration, temperature) leading up to an inspection.

Factory AI excels here by being **sensor-agnostic**. Whether you use existing sensors or new ones, the data feeds directly into the compliance log, bridging the gap between *asset health* and *regulatory reporting*.

### Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: How long must maintenance records be kept in Australia?**
**A:** Under WHS Regulation 237, maintenance records for a plant must be kept for the entire period the person has management or control of the plant. If you sell the equipment, you must transfer these records to the new owner. For registered plant (like pressure vessels), records often need to be kept for the life of the asset.

**Q: Is a digital logbook legal for WHS compliance in Australia?**
**A:** Yes, and it is preferred. The regulations require records to be "kept," but do not mandate paper. Digital records are superior because they are searchable, timestamped, and backed up. **Factory AI** provides a compliant digital framework that meets the accessibility requirements of the WHS Act.

In the event of an accident, lack of records can be used as evidence of negligence, potentially leading to prosecution under industrial manslaughter laws in states like Victoria and Queensland.
